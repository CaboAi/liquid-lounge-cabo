## What Exactly Is NAD+ and Why Does Your Body Need It?

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every cell of every living organism. Research published in PLOS ONE (2012) found that NAD+ levels in human skin drop more than 50% from newborn to young adulthood, then fall an additional 60% from young adult to middle age. That's a massive biological shift most people never know is happening. ([PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0042357), 2012)

Think of NAD+ as the molecule that makes everything else possible inside a cell. It sits at the center of three critical biological processes. First, it's required for mitochondrial energy production: your mitochondria use NAD+ to convert nutrients into ATP, the actual fuel cells run on. When NAD+ drops, ATP production drops with it. That's where the fatigue comes from.

Second, NAD+ activates a family of proteins called sirtuins. Sirtuins regulate inflammation, control how genes are expressed, and influence how quickly cells age. They need NAD+ to function at all. Low NAD+ means quieter sirtuins, which means faster cellular aging.

Third, your DNA repair enzymes (called PARP enzymes) consume NAD+ every time they fix a break in your DNA. The older you get, the more DNA damage accumulates, and the more PARP pulls from your already-shrinking NAD+ supply. It becomes a difficult cycle to break on its own.

## Why Do NAD+ Levels Decline With Age?

NAD+ decline isn't a single process. It's the result of three separate forces working at the same time, and all three accelerate with age. Liver biopsy samples from adults averaging age 66 showed roughly 30% lower NAD+ than samples from adults averaging age 39. Spinal fluid from individuals over 45 showed a 14% decline compared to those under 45. ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32097708/), 2020)

The first force is an enzyme called CD38. CD38 becomes significantly more active as you age, particularly in the presence of chronic low-grade inflammation. Its job is to consume NAD+. The older and more inflamed your tissue environment, the more CD38 activity, and the faster NAD+ disappears.

The second force is NAMPT, the enzyme your body relies on most heavily to synthesize new NAD+. NAMPT activity decreases with age. So at the exact same time CD38 is destroying more NAD+, your body is producing less of it. The math compounds quickly.

The third force is accumulated DNA damage. As PARP enzymes work overtime repairing decades of cellular wear, they drain NAD+ faster than your synthesis pathways can keep up. The result shows up in organ tissue, brain tissue, and muscle. You feel it as fatigue, cognitive slowdown, and sluggish recovery after exercise.

## Does a Blood Test Actually Reveal Your NAD+ Status?

Here's something almost no one in the wellness space is talking about yet. A 2026 study published in Nature Metabolism measured whole-blood NAD+ in 237 healthy individuals and found it does not decline uniformly with age. Blood NAD+ can look relatively stable on standard lab work. But tissue-level NAD+ in muscle, brain, and fat still declines significantly. ([Nature Metabolism](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42255-026-01540-w), 2026)

This is a critical distinction. If your doctor ordered blood work and your NAD+ came back in a "normal" range, that test may not reflect what's actually happening inside your cells. Blood is a convenient sample. It's not the same as measuring what's happening in your mitochondria or your prefrontal cortex.

This helps explain a question I hear regularly: why do some people feel chronically fatigued and cognitively slow when their standard labs look unremarkable? Cellular depletion at the tissue level may be the gap. A 2024 neuroimaging study using magnetic resonance spectroscopy confirmed that whole-brain NAD+ levels do decline with age, even when other markers look stable. ([NAD.com](https://www.nad.com/news/whole-brain-nad-levels-decline-with-age-new-study-shows), 2024)

## Why Choose IV Delivery Over NMN or NR Supplements?

Oral supplements require conversion. NMN and NR are NAD+ precursors, meaning your body must enzymatically convert them into NAD+ after absorption. A meaningful portion of that conversion is lost in GI transit. For a daily supplement protocol designed to work over months, oral forms make sense and the research supports them. They're also genuinely convenient.

For a traveler in Cabo who wants an acute, session-based result, IV is the relevant format. NAD+ enters the bloodstream directly. There's no waiting for absorption, no GI conversion variable, and no uncertainty about how much actually reached your cells.

That said, it's worth being honest about what the research does and doesn't show. A 2021 clinical study found that IV NAD+ produced significant improvement on 6 out of 8 cognitive performance tests, compared to 2 out of 8 in the saline control group. ([Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003999321011096), 2021) That's a meaningful clinical signal, not a guarantee of identical results for every individual. What it is: solid evidence that IV administration produces measurable cognitive effects. Oral precursor supplementation hasn't matched that in equivalent head-to-head trials.
