## What Is Actually Happening Inside Your Body During a Hangover?

A hangover isn't just "being thirsty." According to the NIAAA, alcohol suppresses vasopressin (ADH), the hormone that tells your kidneys to hold onto water. Without that signal, your kidneys excrete far more fluid than normal, which directly causes the headache, fatigue, and intense thirst you feel the morning after ([NIAAA](https://www.niaaa.nih.gov/publications/brochures-and-fact-sheets/hangovers)). That's the dehydration piece. But it's only one piece.

A hangover is actually multi-causal. Dehydration starts the cascade, but your body is also dealing with electrolyte depletion, B vitamin loss, blood sugar swings, and a surge of acetaldehyde — the toxic byproduct your liver produces while breaking down alcohol. Each of these contributes to a distinct set of symptoms.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **Headache and thirst** - primarily from dehydration and vasopressin suppression
- **Fatigue and brain fog** - B1 (thiamine), B6, and B12 depletion
- **Muscle cramps and weakness** - potassium and magnesium loss
- **Nausea** - acetaldehyde toxicity and gastric inflammation
- **Shakiness** - blood sugar dysregulation

In 10+ years of clinical nursing, I've seen patients come in thinking they just need "a big glass of water." That helps. But when you've been drinking for hours in 95-degree heat, oral fluids alone take time your body doesn't have. The depletion is too significant for a glass of Pedialyte to fully close the gap quickly.

> Alcohol suppresses vasopressin (ADH), causing the kidneys to excrete far more fluid than normal. The NIAAA identifies this mechanism as the direct driver of hangover dehydration, headache, thirst, and fatigue ([NIAAA](https://www.niaaa.nih.gov/publications/brochures-and-fact-sheets/hangovers)).

## Why Does Cabo Make a Hangover So Much Worse?

Cabo San Lucas sits in the Sonoran Desert corridor. Its UV index regularly hits 12-13 — classified as "extreme" by the World Health Organization. Daytime temperatures run 90-95°F through most of the peak tourist season, with 11 or more hours of direct sunlight per day. Drinking alcohol in those conditions accelerates dehydration far faster than the same amount of alcohol consumed in a cool, shaded environment.

Most hangover articles are written by people sitting in temperate climates. They're not accounting for what happens when you're drinking poolside in a desert at noon. Alcohol already causes your body to expel roughly 800-1,000 ml of urine for every 250 ml of alcohol consumed — a 4:1 fluid loss ratio ([Cleveland Clinic](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/does-alcohol-dehydrate-you), 2024). Add direct sun, sweat, and heat-driven fluid loss on top of that, and the dehydration hole you're climbing out of is significantly deeper than it would be anywhere else.

Think about it this way: a tourist in Chicago who drinks four cocktails at a bar goes home to air conditioning. A tourist in Cabo who drinks four cocktails at a swim-up bar has been baking in the sun the entire time. By the time they stop drinking, they may already be behind by a liter or more of fluid before the alcohol's diuretic effect even finishes running its course.

> Alcohol causes the body to expel approximately 800-1,000 ml of urine for every 250 ml of alcohol consumed, a roughly 4:1 fluid loss ratio ([Cleveland Clinic](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/does-alcohol-dehydrate-you), 2024). In Cabo's extreme heat and UV conditions, additional fluid loss from sweating compounds this depletion significantly.

## What Does IV Therapy Actually Fix — And What Doesn't It Fix?

IV therapy for hangover IV therapy in Cabo San Lucas addresses four of the five root causes of a hangover. It does not fix everything — and I'd rather tell you that upfront than overpromise and underdeliver.

**What IV therapy directly addresses:**

IV fluids restore blood volume and hydration status rapidly. A standard 1,000 ml saline drip bypasses your GI tract entirely, which is significant. According to research published in PMC/NIH in 2025, IV delivery achieves 100% bioavailability directly to the bloodstream, while oral absorption of the same nutrients can vary from 10-90% depending on the compound ([PMC/NIH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12182718/)). When you're nauseous and can barely keep water down, that difference matters.

A properly formulated hangover IV also replaces:

- **Electrolytes** (potassium, magnesium, sodium) lost through the diuretic effect of alcohol. Research published in the Medical News Bulletin found that approximately 50% of heavy drinkers show measurable potassium deficiency ([Medical News Bulletin](https://medicalnewsbulletin.com/electrolyte-imbalance-in-alcoholics/)).
- **B vitamins** (B1/thiamine, B6, B12), which alcohol actively depletes and which are essential for energy metabolism and neurological function.
- **Anti-nausea medication** can be added directly to the bag, so you're not fighting to keep oral anti-emetics down.

**What IV therapy does NOT fix:**

Acetaldehyde. That's the toxic byproduct your liver produces while metabolizing ethanol. It's responsible for much of the nausea, flushing, and general misery you feel. IV therapy does not remove acetaldehyde, and it does not speed up your liver's metabolic rate. Your liver processes alcohol at roughly one drink per hour, and no IV drip changes that.

Blood sugar regulation is also partial. An IV containing dextrose can raise blood glucose directly, which helps the shakiness and mental fog. But the full restoration of stable blood sugar takes time and food.

I tell every client the same thing before we start: you're going to feel significantly better, but you may not feel 100% until your liver finishes its job. Most people are surprised by how much better they feel within 45-60 minutes. The dehydration piece is often the dominant driver of how terrible they feel. Fixing that fast makes everything else more manageable.

## How Long Does a Hangover Last Without Treatment?

Without any intervention, the average hangover lasts 18.4 hours ([Open Access Pub](https://openaccesspub.org/addiction-disorder-and-rehabilitation/article/690)). That's nearly a full day. For a tourist with a 72-hour trip to Cabo, losing 18 hours to a hangover means losing 25% of their entire vacation.

Not everyone gets hangovers, either. Research published in PMC in 2022 found that approximately 75-78% of heavy drinkers experience hangover symptoms, while 20-25% are effectively "hangover resistant" ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9482023/)). If you're in the majority who does suffer, the timeline matters a lot.

> Natural hangover recovery without treatment averages 18.4 hours ([Open Access Pub](https://openaccesspub.org/addiction-disorder-and-rehabilitation/article/690)). Approximately 75-78% of heavy drinkers experience hangover symptoms ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9482023/), 2022), making fast recovery options particularly valuable for travelers on limited time.

Most clients who call me are already a few hours into their hangover and losing patience with waiting it out. The honest answer is that IV hydration can meaningfully compress that 18-hour timeline. It won't cut it to zero. But going from 18 hours to 4-6 hours of real recovery time is the difference between missing your snorkeling trip and actually making it.
